Virus vs. Virus

Topics: Types of Malware | Viruses

Date: 21/08/2025

We call them viruses, but not every piece of malicious code is a virus; just as not every illness is viral. The term has become shorthand for all malware, which obscures both biology and computer science. This post explores the similarities between natural and computer viruses, highlights the differences, and explains why the virus metaphor works (and where it fails).

// The Metaphor

Both biological and computer viruses are parasitic replicators. A biological virus has no metabolism of its own; it survives by entering a living cell and hijacking its molecular machinery to copy itself. A computer virus behaves in much the same way, attaching to a host file or boot record and reproducing whenever that host is executed.

This resemblance was visible from the very beginning. The first recognized computer virus, Creeper (1971), spread across ARPANET by copying itself from machine to machine, playfully displaying the message "I'm the Creeper: catch me if you can." It was soon followed by Reaper, the first antivirus, written to remove Creeper. The metaphor of "infection" was irresistible: code was acting like a pathogen, spreading in ways that strongly resembled biological viruses.

// Virus vs. Malware

In cybersecurity, "virus" does not mean the same thing as "malware". Malware is the umbrella term: any malicious software designed to cause harm, disruption, or unauthorized access. Viruses are a specific kind within that family; much like a viral infection is just a specific kind within the family of illness. What makes them distinct is the method of self-replication. They insert themselves into host files or boot records and spread only when those infected hosts are executed.

That is not how all malware behaves. Worms spread without needing host files, jumping across networks on their own. Trojans pose as legitimate software and wait for the user to install and execute them (metaphorically comparable with a carnivorous plant, waiting for an insect to pollinate it and then eating it). Ransomware holds data hostage. Spyware hides quietly in the system to collect information and report back to a beacon.

Brain (1986), often cited as the first PC virus "in the wild", spread via floppy disks by infecting boot sectors. Compare that to ILOVEYOU (2000), which was not a virus but a worm: it spread independently through email without needing a host file. Yet because viruses were among the earliest and most visible threats, their name became shorthand for all malicious code; much as "flu" became a catch-all term for colds, fevers, and respiratory infections in casual speech.

In a nutshell, computer viruses are to malware as the viral infections are to diseases.

// The Differences

The metaphor also breaks down in important ways. Biological viruses are products of evolution: they emerge from mutation and selection, not design. Computer viruses are engineered, often deliberately crafted as experiments, pranks, or weapons. Even polymorphic malware that mutates its code is still ultimately constrained by human intent.

The vectors differ as well. Biology uses air, blood, surfaces, and vectors like mosquitoes. Computing uses infected files, removable media, network protocols, and user actions like clicking a malicious attachment. Both rely on transmission pathways, but their environments could not be more different.

// How Computer Viruses Really Work

The life cycle of a computer virus is simple. First, it embeds itself into a legitimate host file or boot sector. Next, when the host runs, the virus executes too, spreading by copying itself into new files. Finally, it may unleash a payload, which can range from a harmless prank to destructive file corruption or data theft.

As antivirus tools improved, viruses became stealthier. Some used polymorphism, mutating their code each time they replicated to evade signature detection. Others became metamorphic, rewriting their own logic while preserving function. Some delayed their activity until a specific date (a notorious example being the Michelangelo virus (1992)), which activated on the author's birthday and threatened to destroy data worldwide (in reality, it caused way less damage than feared, but the panic cemented the virus metaphor in the public imagination).

// Lessons From Biology

Despite the differences, biology still provides useful lessons. Patching software is akin to vaccination: it closes a pathway before infection can take hold. Quarantine in computing (sandboxing or network isolation) mirrors the biological principle of containing the infected before they can spread. Even the idea of herd immunity has a digital equivalent in network hygiene: when most machines are patched, epidemics are less likely.

The parallel also encompasses the arms race. Just as pathogens evolve under immune pressure, malware evolves under defensive pressure. Each new defensive measure provides a fitness test for the adversary. But here's the critical point: modern malware rarely spreads this way anymore. Network worms, Trojans, and ransomware dominate real-world attacks; viruses are now more historical than practical. Which makes the metaphor to biology even more poetic: the computer virus, like many natural ones, has been largely erradicated by meticulous human prevention.

// Where the Metaphor Ends

Unlike nature, digital viruses don't "emerge" without human intent. Treating viruses (or malware in general) as we treat mindless biological agents risks naturalizing what is most of the time ultimately human aggression expressed in code. Worms don't spread because of some biological need to do it, they spread because someone wrote them to do so. Defenses must address not just the artifact (malware) but also understand (and occasionally bring accountable) its author.

// Final Thoughts

All viruses are malware, but not all malware are viruses. The metaphor to biology is helpful for understanding replication and spread, but it can mislead if taken too literally. In today's landscape, worms, Trojans, and especially ransomware dominate, yet the word virus endures because replication was the first great fear in cybersecurity. Biology still offers a vivid analogy, but computer science has evolved far beyond the coinage of the term. What remains most important is the presence of intent: biological viruses emerge blindly, but digital ones are written with purpose.

Both kinds of virus exploit hosts to survive, but only one is a product of human design. To defend against malware, we must think not only like biologists studying pathogens but also like strategists anticipating adversaries.
